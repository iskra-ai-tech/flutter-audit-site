02b Free · Read on the page

The 12-point Flutter performance checklist.

The exact warm-pass I run against every codebase on Day 1, before I touch DevTools. Public on purpose. If your team can clear all twelve, you probably do not need me.

  1. 01

    Const constructors everywhere they belong.

    Every widget that does not depend on dynamic state should be const. Non-const subtrees rebuild on every parent setState; const subtrees skip the diff entirely. Grep: final\s+\w+\s*=\s*Widget without const.

  2. 02

    RepaintBoundary around every animated subtree.

    Animating children without a RepaintBoundary repaints the parent layer on every frame. Free 30–40% paint cost on animated screens. Check: the Flutter DevTools Performance Overlay red bars.

  3. 03

    Shader warm-up on first frame.

    First-run jank is almost always shader compilation. Pre-compile with --cache-sksl-path on iOS, ship the warm cache, or pre-paint signature widgets on a hidden frame. Symptom: the first scroll is jankier than the second.

  4. 04

    Lists ship via builder or slivers.

    Eager ListView(children: [ … ]) mounts every child on first frame. Above ~12 items you are paying for content that has never been on screen. Fix: always ListView.builder or a CustomScrollView of slivers.

  5. 05

    Images decode at display size, not source size.

    A 2000×2000 hero rendered at 200×200 still decodes 16 MB of pixels on the main thread. Fix: cacheWidth/cacheHeight on Image, ResizeImage, and precacheImage for known-soon assets.

  6. 06

    No side effects inside build().

    No setState, no provider notifications, no async kick-offs. build runs unpredictably often; side effects there are the source of half of the rebuild storms I find.

  7. 07

    State scope is the smallest leaf, not the whole screen.

    A setState in HomeScreenState rebuilds the entire home screen. Fix: ValueListenableBuilder, Selector on a Provider, or a small inner StatefulBuilder.

  8. 08

    Providers and notifiers do not over-broadcast.

    Top-level ChangeNotifier with notifyListeners() in a hot path rebuilds every listener in the tree. Fix: split state, use Selector, or replace with a Riverpod StateNotifier with narrower outputs.

  9. 09

    Animation controllers are disposed.

    Every AnimationController needs a matching dispose. Leaked tickers keep firing for the life of the app — invisible to the user, lethal to long sessions on mid-tier Android.

  10. 10

    CustomPainter.shouldRepaint says no by default.

    The default is repaint always. Override shouldRepaint to return false when nothing relevant changed. Free win on any signature paint.

  11. 11

    No async bursts in initState.

    Multiple awaited futures in initState stall the first frame. Fix: kick them off without awaiting, render a skeleton, fill in as data arrives. The first frame is the impression.

  12. 12

    Build graph depth under ~20 in hot paths.

    Every layer is a Element diff per rebuild. A 35-deep widget tree in a list item is the silent tax that turns a 60 FPS screen into a 38 FPS one. Fix: hoist subtrees behind const or extract them to siblings.

If two or more of these are violated in your codebase, the audit usually pays for itself in the first PR you do not have to write.

03 How I read code

A code audit is not a code review.

A code review tells you what is syntactically wrong. An audit tells you what is strategically wrong. Which decision from 2023 is costing a week per release. Which custom animation will bite you on the next Flutter version. Which third of your providers can be deleted.

A linter sees code; the audit sees decisions. Three days is enough because the diagnosis is the hard part. The fix list is short on purpose. Most of the value is what is no longer there.

06 Questions

Six questions, six answers.

Why an audit instead of just hiring a senior Flutter engineer?

Hiring takes three to four months and a salary. The audit takes three days and $500. If after the audit you decide you do need to hire, you will know exactly what skills the hire needs, which is usually the harder question. The audit is the cheapest way to find out what hiring will not fix.

Do you need the source code?

Yes. The audit is me reading your code. Walkthroughs, screen-shares, recordings, or descriptions are not a substitute — I need the repo. NDA before code changes hands, no problem. If the source cannot be shared, the audit is not a fit.

Why $500 — is it a real audit at that price?

Yes. It is intentionally priced as a low-friction first engagement. You get a senior engineer for three days, a real report, and a walkthrough — the same shape as a $5,000 audit, scoped tighter. If the work runs over, that is my problem, not yours. About half the audits I run lead to a deeper engagement after; most do not, and that is fine. The $500 is the deliverable, not a foot in the door.

Our app is not slow. It is just messy. Is the audit still useful?

Yes, possibly more so. Performance audits get the headlines, architectural audits change quarters. If your team is touching eight files for a one-field change, you do not have a performance problem, you have a velocity problem. Same audit, different fix-list categories.

How is this different from running flutter analyze or a code-quality SaaS?

flutter analyze tells you what is syntactically wrong. The audit tells you what is strategically wrong. A linter sees code. I see decisions.

What if you find the problem is too big for a 3-day audit?

I tell you on Day 2, refund in full, and write up what I have found so far in whatever shape is useful to you. The 3-day audit is sized to diagnose, not to rewrite. If your problem genuinely needs ten engineer-days, the report says so on Day 2 and we both move on.

Do you do the fixes too?

Usually no. The audit is the product. Most teams find their own engineers ship the fix list faster than I could, and they learn the codebase along the way. For unusual cases (a custom shader to be written, a vector_math_64 rewrite of a gesture system), I do take implementation work, but only after the audit, and only if you ask.
